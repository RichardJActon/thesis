The Epigenomics of Human Ageing

Chapter 7 Introduction

Age is a significant risk factor across the breadth of chronic diseases, including cardiovascular, obesity-related, ophthalmic, musculoskeletal and neurological disorders [195]. The molecular changes that occur with age include alterations to the epigenome [77] – the chemical modifications and packaging of the genome that indicate or facilitate cell-specific activity [16]. This includes age-related changes to DNA methylation, the most common modification of DNA [76]. This epigenetic mechanism is crucial in repressing the large proportion of the human genome (~45% [286]) that is comprised of repetitive or transposable elements [287], [288]. It also classically leads to robust repression of gene expression through promoter DNA methylation [289], and has more nuanced effects on expression within the gene body [290], as well as influencing splicing [291] and transcription factor binding-sites (TFBSs) [292], [52].

A significant decrease in DNA methylation with age was first observed with pioneering global measures [78], in similar fashion to the reduced methylation seen in cancer tissue [293]. This DNA methylation loss was seen to be strongly contributed to by the demethylation of repetitive elements [294]. Random drift in the epigenetic state with age was also observed [294], and more recent studies have identified targeted hypermethylation of specific functional regions, such as the promoters of polycomb target genes [96], bivalent chromatin domains [94] and the genomic loci of specific tRNA genes [193]

Alu elements are the most successful transposon in terms of numbers within the human genome, totalling ~1.19 million copies and comprising ~10.7% of the genomic sequence [295], [296]. This ~300 bp primate-specific retrotransposon is a member of the short interspersed nuclear elements (SINEs) and is derived from the 7SL RNA gene [297]. Alu elements arose ~65 million years ago (MYA), with a peak in amplification around ~40 MYA [298]. Non-LTR retrotransposons, which include Alus as well as L1 and SVA elements, are active in human. However, Alu elements themselves are non-autonomous and need to commandeer a nearby L1 element’s retrotransposition molecular machinery [299]. Alu insertion mutagenesis as well as Alu element mediated recombination account for 0.4% of all human genetic disorders [288], [300].

DNA methylation represses Alu elements and due to their sequences being rich in CpGs they in fact account for ~25% of all the CpGs in human genome (~7.5 million) [296]. Alu repeats are predominately methylated across all normal tissues, and even in cancer cells they are the more resistant to hypomethylation than other repetitive elements, implying a strong pressure to maintain their epigenetic state [301]. These elements are an abundant genetic resource of regulatory sequences with numerous TFBSs residing within its canonical sequence [302]. They can influence gene expression [303], [304] and there is evidence that some may possess enhancer activity [305]. The deterioration of epigenomic repression can unmask latent regulatory function within repetitive elements [306], [197], [307]. This has been clearly displayed as a pathogenic mechanism in cancer, with epigenetic reactivation of cryptic cis-regulatory elements (CREs) within transposons acting as oncogenic drivers [308] , including Alu elements [309]. There is the potential for these changes to also influence common non-malignant age-related diseases [197], [310].

The identification of specific age-related DNA methylation changes has enabled the construction of ‘DNA methylation clocks‘ [134] that can predict an individual’s chronological age, but also capture aspects of biological ageing, such as all-cause mortality [120]. Hypermethylated and hypomethylated cytosines approximately equally drive these DNA methylation clocks, although these age-related increases or decreases in methylation are driven by distinct biological mechanisms [311], [61]. Interestingly, hypomethylating CpGs have been identified to be the most indicative of biological ageing-related all-cause mortality effects in blood [125]. However, due to poor coverage of the repetitive space because of technical issues [312], much of the genomic hypomethylation is not well captured by array analysis.

In this study we set about to identify and explore age-related DNA methylation changes in Alu elements in a large (>3,000) immunoprecipitation-derived sequencing-based dataset (MeDIP-seq) from peripheral blood DNA. We constructed an Alu-specific DNA methylation ageing clock and compared how this contrasted with other clocks’ measures. We also investigated genetic influences on age- acceleration measures from this Alu-clock via GWAS, as has been with shown previously with an association in the TERT gene with the Horvath clock [313], [314]. These results may reveal mechanisms involved in controlling the epigenetic state of this large portion of the genome. Finally, we interrogated these genetic associations for any potential connection with common age-related diseases through PheWAS in the UKBiobank dataset [315].
